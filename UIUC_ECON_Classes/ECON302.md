#### Change in Demand
A rightward or leftward shift of the entire demand curve for a good that is caused by a change in something other than the price of that good
For example, an increase in the number of exams shifted the entire demand curve for coffee to the right
#### Law of Demand(Version2)
An increase in the price of Good X will cause the quantity demanded of Good x to fall and a decrease in the price of Good X will cause the quantity demanded of Good X to rise, ceteris paribus
`Ceteris Paribus` - holding all else constant

Expansion in Demand -- shift rightward, demand a higher quantity at any given price
Contraction in Demand -- shift leftward, demand a lower quantity at any given price
Change in Quantity Demanded -- A movement along a stationary demand curve of a good due to a change in the price of that good

#### Common Causes of Demand Shifts
##### Ceteris Paribus Conditions
1. Consumer's income
2. Prices of related goods
3. Tastes/quality
4. Consumer expectations
5. Number of buys

##### Normal Good
- When consumers get **richer**, demand for a normal good **expands**
- When consumers get **poorer**, demand for a normal good **contracts**

##### Inferior Good
- When consumers get **richer**, demand for an inferior good **contracts**
- When consumers get **poorer**, demand for an inferior good **expands**

##### Complements
- When the price of Good A **rises** the demand for Good B **contracts**
- When the price of Good A **falls** the demand for Good B **expands**

##### Substitutes
- When the price of Good X **rises** demand for Good Y **expands**
- When the price of Good X **falls** demand for Good Y **contracts**

##### Consumer Expectations
- If new information is revealed and consumers believe the future price will be **higher** than they previously thought, the demand today will **expand**
- If new information is revealed and consumers believe the future price will be **lower** than they previously thought, the demand today will **contract**

##### Number of Consumers
- At any given price, if the size of the population of consumers **grows**, then the demand will **expand** 
- At any given price, if the size of the population of consumers **falls**, then the demand will **contract** 

##### Everything Else
- If willingness to pay **rises**, then demand **expands**
- If willingness to pay **falls**, then demand **contracts**

#### Changes in Demand
##### Expansion
- Increase in income for a normal good 
- Decrease in income for an inferior good
- Increase in the price of a substitute
- Decrease in the price of a complement
- Expecting the price to rise in the future
- Increase in the number of consumers

##### Contraction
- Decrease in income for a normal good
- Increase in income for an inferior good
- Decrease in the price of a substitute
- Increase in the price of a complement
- Expecting the price to fall in the future
- Decrease in the number of consumers

### Lesson 2.03 Single-Variable Supply

There is a positive relationship between the price of a good and the quantity supplied of that good

#### Supply Function
Mathematical relationship where quantity supplied is a function of price

A line or curve that shows the quantity supplied of a good over a range of possible prices

#### Marginal Cost
The change in total cost that producers incur when they produce an additional unit
"marginal" means "change in"

#### Law of Escalating Marginal Cost
Also called the last of increasing marginal cost

The proposition that as quantity produced increases the marginal cost also increases

For example, if the first unit will add 1 dollar, the second will add more about 1 dollar

#### Profit Maximization Rule 
Competitive firms will compare the price they can sell at to their marginal cost

Any time the extra cost of producing another unit is less than the price, then producing that unit will increase their profit

#### Summary
- Supply curves slop upward showing the escalating marginal costs of production
- The supply curve can tell you the quantity supplied at a given price or the height can tell you the marginal cost of given unit of the good
- Remember: the height of the supply curve already tells you the change in total cost, you will NOT need to find the change in the height to find marginal cost

An increase in the price of Good X will cause the quantity supplied of Good X to rise and a decrease in the price of Good X will cause the quantity supplied of Good X to fall, ceteris paribus

#### Common causes of supply shifts

1. input prices
2. production technology 
3. producer expectations
4.  number of sellers

##### Input Prices
- Input price refers to a price of a good that is used to produce another good
- Input prices **rise** which causes supply to **contract** 
- Input prices **fall** which causes supply to **expand**

##### Production Technology
- When production technology **improves** supply **expands**
- When production technology **worsens** or is **restricted**, then supply **contracts**

##### Producer Expectations
- If new information is revealed and producers believe the future price will be **higher** than they previously through, the supply today will **contract** as they wait to sell at the higher price
- If new information is revealed and producers believe the future price will be **lower** than they previously through, the supply today will **expand** as they wait to sell at the higher price

##### Number of Suppliers
If additional suppliers **enter** the market, then supply will **expand**
If suppliers **exit** the market, then supply will **contract**

##### Everything Else
If Marginal Cost **falls**, this causes Supply to **expand**
If Marginal Cost **rises**, this causes Supply to **contract**

#### Changes in Supply
##### Expansion
- Decrease in input price

- Reduction of Marginal Costs

- Increase of Marginal Costs

### Lesson 2.05 Equilibrium
#### Surplus
At the current price, the quantity supplied is **greater** than the quantity demanded
Surpluses will tend to drive prices **down**

#### Shortage
At the current price, the quantity supplied is **less** than the quantity demanded
Shortages will tend to drive prices **up**

#### Market-Clearing Price
The price at which quantity demanded is equal to quantity supplied

#### Equilibrium
There is no upward pressure or downward pressure on price

#### Summary
- Price is the outcome of adjustments caused by shortages and surpluses.
- The market will â€œgravitateâ€ to a combination of price and quantity where the market clears. 
- If there is a change to demand or supply, the market will gravitate to a new combination of price and quantity.

### Lesson 2.06 Comparative Statics
#### Comparative Statics
A process where we compare the price and quantity at an initial equilibrium to the price and quantity of a new equilibrium after demand or supply has changed

1. Start in equilibrium
2. Change a determining factor
3. Determine the impact on Demand or Supply
4. Finish up in a new equilibrium

#### FLOPs

#### Price Gouging
A pejorative term for when suppliers raise price above what is deemed "fair"

#### Summary
- Consider the effect of an outside factor on the market you are analyzing. The outside factor may cause a change in demand or a change in supply or both.
- If both demand and supply change, then the change in price or the change in quantity will be uncertain.
- Laws that prevent prices from rising will tend to have unintended consequences of prolonging shortages.

### Lesson 4.02 Budget Constraints
#### Budget Constraint
The boundaries in consumption that individuals face as a result of limited incomes
##### Budget Line Formula
M =$P_X$X + $P_Y$Y

#### Market Rate of Substitution
MktRS is the rate at which one good can be traded for another; the absolute value of the slope of the budget line
##### Formula
$MktRS_{XY}$ = $P_X$ / $P_Y$

#### Summary
- The budget line is a visual representation of the possible combinations of two goods that a consumer is able to purchase when spending their entire budget
- The market rate of substitution(MktRS) is how much of Good Y the consumer gives up to buy one unit of Good X and is the absolute value of the slope of the budget line
- Holding all elese constant, reductions in the price of Good X will shift the X-intercept to the right while the Y-intercept stays the same
- Holding all else constant, reductions in the consumer's income will shift both the X and Y intercepts towards the origin but the slope of the budget line will stay the same

### Lesson 4.03 Utility Maximization
#### Optimizing Condition
Utility is highest where:
1. Slope of the indifference curve = slope of the budget line
2. Marginal Rate of Substitution = Market Rate of Substitution
3. $MU_X$ / $MU_Y$ = $P_X$ / $P_Y$

#### Summary 
- We assume the consumer's goal is to maximize utility subject to their budget constraint. In other words, choose the bundle on their budget line that has the highest utility
- If possible, the combination of Good X and Good Y on a budget constraint that maximizes utility will be the point where $MRS_{XY}$ = $MktRS_{XY}$

### Lesson 4.04 Utility Maximization Examples
#### Corner Solution
A situation in which the marginal rate of substitution of one good for another cannot be equal for non-negative quantities of both goods
The consumer will choose to spend their entire budget on only Good X or only Good Y whichever brings the highest utility

$MRS_{XY}$ -- How much of Good Y the consumer is willing to give up to get a unit of Good X
$MktRS_{XY}$ -- How much of Good Y the consumer has to give up to get a unit of Good X

#### Summary 
- Usually, we can use the optimization rule: $MRS_{XY}$ = $MktRS_{XY}$ to solve for the utility-maximizing bundle of Good X and Y
- If not, we have other strategies:
	1. More Good X if $MRS_{XY}$ > $MktRS_{XY}$
	2. Less Good X if $MRS_{XY}$ < $MktRS_{XY}$

### Lesson 5.1 Income and Substitution Effects
#### Why does consumers buy less goods when the price rises
Increasing in price means that buying a unit of one good needs to giving up more units of other good
	As a result, one good become relatively more expensive and another one become relatively less expensive
	And a consumer will substitute away from the expensive good and toward cheaper good

#### Substitution Effect
The change in the amount of Good X consumed associated with a change in the price of Good X, with utility held constant

Since one of our goods became more expensive, the dollars the consumer has will purchase less. It is like they became **poorer**
A consumer will change buying behavior when their **income** changes

#### Income Effect
The change in the amount of Good X consumed associated with a change in consumer's purchasing power, with relative prices held constant

> The price change create both an Income Effect and a Substitution Effect simultaneously

#### Compensated Budget Line
1. Uses the new relative prices
2. A new level of income to compensate for any changes to purchasing power and 
3. Is tangent to the original indifference curve
#### How to Find the Compensated Budget Line
1. FInd $MRS_{XY}$
	1. $MU_X$
	2. $MU_Y$
	3. $MRS_{XY} = \frac{MU_X}{MU_Y}$
2. Calculate $MktRS_{XY} = \frac{P_X}{P_Y}$
3. Set $MRS_{XY} = MktRS_{XY}$ and then solve the optimization condition for Y
4. Calculate utility from original bundle
5. Substitute the level of utility from Step 4 and Y from Step 3 into the utility function then solve for X
6. Use X with the optimization condition from Step 3 to find Y
Suppose X is the good that raised the price
Total Effect: New X - Original X
Substitution Effect: Compensated X - Original X
Income Effect: New X - Compensated X

#### Summary
- The sum of the income and substitution effects is the total effect of a price change
- A compensated budget line compensated for changes to purchasing power. It eliminates the income effect and leaves only the substitution effect
- If $P_X$ rises, then the compensation will be to increase income. If $P_X$ falls, then the compensation will be to reduce income

### Lesson 07.01 Production Functions
#### Factors of Production (Inputs)
1. **Labors** -- the number of workers or the number of hours worked by people. Variable: **L**
2. **Capital** -- also called physical capital. Machines, equipment, buildings and tools used in the production process. Variable: **K**
3. **Human Capital** -- Knowledge and experience of workers
4. Other inputs: Materials, Land

#### Production Function
A function showing the highest output that a firm can produce for every specified combination of inputs
Functional Form: q = f(L, K)
Lowercase 'q' represents output of an individual firm
"Output(q) depends on the amount of labor and capital used"
#### Law of Marginally Diminishing Returns
Principle that as the use of an input increase, with other inputs fixed, the resulting additions to output will eventually decrease. Also called **Diminishing Marginal Returns**

#### Cobb-Douglas Production Function
General Functional Form:
q = $aL^cK^d$
Values can be used for parameters a, c and d to fit a particular the production technology and productivity of a specific industry

#### Short Run and Long Run
**Short Run** -- Period of time where one input is considered to be fixed
Technically, capital(size of the factory) are considered as fixed , since it is easier to change the size of labor
**Long Run** -- Period of time where all inputs can be changed

#### Marginal Product of Labor ($MP_L$)
The change in output that is the result of increasing the amount of labor used by 1 unit

##### Marginal Product of Labor($MP_L$) Function
The change in output for a change in labor while holding other inputs constant. The partial derivative of the production function with respect to Labor
$$MP_L = \frac{\partial q}{\partial L}$$
#### Marginal Product of Capital (MPK) Function
The change in output for a change in capital while holding other inputs constant
The partial derivative of the production function with respect to Capital
$$MP_K = \frac{\partial q}{\partial K}$$
#### Average Product of Labor Function($AP_L$)
Total product divided by the amount of labor
$$AP_L = \frac{q}{L}$$
Both $MP_L$ and $AP_L$ diminish as labor is increased. However, $AP_L$ is always greater than $MP_L$ for any given level of labor

#### Summary
- Total product of labor curves(holding capital constant) will increase at a decreasing rate because of marginally diminishing returns to labor
- Marginal Product of Labor(and Capital) curves represent the slope of their total product curves and will slop downward
- Increasing Capital increases both total product of labor and marginal product of labor

### Lesson 07.02: Returns to Scale
#### Law of Marginally Diminishing Returns
Principle that as the use of an input increases, with other inputs fixed, the resulting additions to output will eventually decrease

#### Returns to Scale
The change in total output when changing both labor and capital in equal proportions
- **Constant Returns to Scale**: Output changes by the same proportion that labor and. capital is changed. For instance, doubling both labor and capital doubles output
- **Increasing Returns to Scale**: Output changes more than the proportion that labor and capital is changed. For instance, doubling both labor and capital more than doubles output
- **Decreasing Returns to Scale**: Output changes less than the proportion that labor and capital is changed. For instance, doubling both labor and capital less than doubles output
#### Cobb-Douglas Parameters and Returns to Scale
Cobb-Douglas General Function Form: 
$$ q = aL^cK^d$$
**Constant Returns to Scale:** c+d = 1
**Increasing Returns to Scale:** c+d > 1
**Decreasing Returns to Scale:** c+d < 1

#### Summary
- Generally, you can test for returns to scale by trying a given level of labor and capital and then doubling them
- As companies get bigger, they may be able to have workers specialize which increases productivity. This would mean increasing returns to scale 
- Companies that get bigger may find management harder. This would lead to decreasing returns to scale

### Lesson 07.03 Isoquants
#### Isoquant
A curve showing all the possible combinations of inputs that yield a given amount of output

#### Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution (MRTS)
The amount by which one input can be reduced when one extra unit of another input is used
$$MRTS_{LK} = \frac{MP_L}{MP_K}$$
$MRTS_{LK}$ -- How much capital can I give up when I hire one more unit of labor and still maintain the same level of output
$MP_L$ -- How productive is another of labor
$MP_K$-- How productive was the capital
For example, if $MRTS_{LK}$ = 5, we can say that we can replace 5 units of capital by 1 unit of labor

#### Cobb-Douglas Shortcut for $MRTS_{LK}$
All Cobb-Douglas Utility functions create a similar result for the $MRTS_{LK}$ calculation
$q = aL^cK^d$
$MRTS_{LK} = \frac{c}{d} \times \frac{K}{L}$

#### Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution ($MRTS_{KL}$)
The amount by which one input can be reduced when one extra unit of another input is used
$$MRTS_{KL} = \frac{MP_K}{MP_L}$$
$MRTS_{LK}$ -- How much labor can I give up when I hire one more unit of capital and still maintain the same level of output
$MP_L$ -- How productive is another of labor
$MP_K$-- How productive was the capital

#### Fixed-Proportions Production Function
- A production function with L-shaped isoquants. MRTS is either infinite or zero
- Input are combined in specific combinations and the ratio of one input to another does not change
- Also known as Leontief Production Function

#### Perfect Substitutes Production Function
- A production function where the MRTS is constant at all points
- Isoquants are linear
- The term, "perfect substitutes," does not mean you can exchange inputs 1-for-1. The important thing is that you can make some substitution over and over without changing the ratio

#### Summary
- Isoquants visualize all the possible input combinations that yield a given level of output
- The absolute value of the slope of an isoquant tells you the amount of the input on the Y-axis you can give up when you add one more unit of the input on the X-axis

#### Explicit versus Implicit Cost
**Explicit Costs:** Observable expenses for a firm or person. These costs involve giving a sum of money to another party

#### Accounting Cost versus Economic Cost
**Accounting Costs** The total of all explicit costs
**Economic Costs** The total of all explicit and implicit cost

**Accounting Profit:** Accounting revenue minus accounting cost
**Economic Profit:** Total revenue minus accounting cost and any implicit cost

#### Sunk Cost
A past cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered
Once a cost is sunk, only future costs are relevant to a decision

#### Sunk Cost Fallacy
When someone makes a decision based on past fixed costs rather than on future costs and benefits

#### Summary

**Total Cost:** The sum of all the costs for a given level of output

### Lesson 09.05 Profit in the Long Run
`Accounting Costs:` The total of all explicit costs
`Economic Costs:` The total of all explicit and implicit cost

`Free Entry:` When there are not barriers to entry
`Barriers to Entry: `Legal restrictions that prevent a firm from opening or increase the cost of opening

#### Profits in the Long Run
- Firms enter into markets with positive economic profit
- The extra production from these firms lower prices and thus lower economic profit
- This continues until economic profit is zero and no additional firms want to enter this market

- Firms exit from markets with negative economic profit
- The lower production from these firms raises price and thus raises economic profit
- This continues until economic profit is zero and no additional firms want to exit this market

`Normal Profit:` There is zero economic profit because accounting profit in every industry is the same. Investing in other industries will not gain a higher accounting return

`Excess Profit(Abnormal Profit): `A situation in which the economic profit is positive

#### Short Run ATC versus Long Run ATC
The minimums of short run ATC sketch out the long run ATC
If a firm can increase output and lower long run ATC, it is called an economy of scale
If a firm increases output and increase the long run ATC, it is called a diseconomy of scale

The quantity associated with the minimum of Long Run ATC is called the optimal firm size($q_{opt}$)
This quantity takes advantage of all economies of scale, but does not go into the diseconomy of scale range

#### Number of firms in the long run:
$$ n = \frac{Q_D}{q_{opt}}$$

`Constant-Cost Industry:` If new firms can enter the market, and all new firms have the same cost functions as old firms, then the Long Run Supply Curve is perfectly elastic

`Increasing-Cost Industry:` If new firms can enter the market, and that drives up input prices then average total costs will increase and the long run supply will be upward-sloping

`Decreasing-Cost Industry:` If new firms enter the market that leads to some economy of scale in the production of inputs which lowers average total costs and leads to a downward-sloping long run supply
This is a very unusual case, and it does not realistically describe many industries

#### Summary
- In the long run, profits cause firms to enter and prices and economics profit to slowly falls to zero
- In the long run, losses cause firms to exit and price and economic profits to rise to zero
- In the long run with free entry and identical firms, the price will be equal to the minimum of long run average total cost 
- At that price, each firm produces a quantity equal to the optimal firm size

#### Summary
- Shortage and surpluses cause price to adjust until we reach equilibrium
- A competitive equilibrium is efficient

### Lesson 11.02 Tax Burden

Percentage of tax paid by demanders versus suppliers

An estimate of the percentage of a tax what will be passed through to the buyers

Tax burden is divided between demanders and suppliers

If demanders do not react to the price increase at all, then they will bear 100% of the tax burden

Perfectly Elastic Suppliers
If suppliers are infinitely price sensitive, they will pass 100% of the tax on to demanders

If suppliers cannot change the quantity they produce in response to a change in the price they receive, then they wil bear 100% of the tax burden

Increasing taxes from zero will cause tax revenue to increase up until some point after which continuing to raise taxes will cause tax revenue to fall

Taxing goods with either inelastic demand or supply will cause the least deadweight loos and generate the most tax revenue

#### Summary
The way in which tax burden is split between demanders and suppliers is based on their relative elasticity
Increasing tax rates causes deadweight loss to increase at an increasing rate
Increasing taxes rates causes tax revenue to rise up until some point at which additional tax increasing cause tax revenue to fall
